Cozy mysteries are having a moment. Richard Osman's Thursday Murder Club brought the genre to mainstream bestseller lists, Nita Prose's *The Maid* proved it could earn serious literary attention, and readers everywhere are discovering (or rediscovering) that there's a very particular pleasure in a murder mystery where the detective drinks tea and the setting is a quiet English village.

But cozy mystery has been around for over a century, Agatha Christie essentially invented it, and the best of the genre stretches well beyond any one book or trend. Here's the essential reading list.

---

## What Makes a Cozy Mystery?

A cozy mystery is a crime story where the darkness is kept at a distance. Violence happens off the page. The detective is usually an amateur. The setting tends to be small and community-focused — a village, a retirement home, a bookshop. There's almost always humour. And crucially, there's warmth: these are books you want to curl up with, not ones that keep you awake at night.

The genre has its conventions, but the best writers use them with intelligence and occasionally subvert them entirely.

## The Essential Classics

### Agatha Christie — Miss Marple series (from 1930)

![A Murder is Announced](https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/71E1IV89mYL._SL1500_.jpg)

Christie invented the template. Miss Marple — elderly, underestimated, frighteningly perceptive — is the original amateur sleuth, and the novels built around her remain among the tightest, most satisfying mysteries ever written.

Start with *The Murder at the Vicarage* (1930) for the first full novel, or *A Murder is Announced* (1950) if you want Christie at the very peak of her powers. ### Alexander McCall Smith — The No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency (from 1998)

Set in Botswana, this series follows Precious Ramotswe, the country's only female private detective. It's one of the most unusual cozy mystery series ever written, unhurried, philosophical, deeply rooted in its place, and Ramotswe herself is one of the most beloved characters in the genre.

The books resist the usual conventions. There's rarely a murder. The cases are small, human, domestic. The pleasure is entirely in spending time with a person of extraordinary wisdom and warmth. Start at the beginning and work through.

## Modern Classics

### Richard Osman — The Thursday Murder Club (from 2020)

![The Thursday Murder Club](https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/71zrZf-nvTL._SL1500_.jpg)

Four retirees in a Kent retirement village meet weekly to discuss cold cases — until a real murder lands on their doorstep. The fastest-selling debut crime novel in UK history, and for good reason.

Osman writes with warmth and genuine wit, and the characters, particularly Elizabeth and Joyce, are among the best in contemporary crime fiction. The series is now five books long and still going. ### Louise Penny — Chief Inspector Gamache series (from 2005)

![Still Life — Louise Penny](https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/81QgJYzFJ3L._SL1500_.jpg)

Set in the fictional Quebec village of Three Pines, this series follows the methodical, principled Chief Inspector Armand Gamache through murders that are as much about character and community as they are about plot.

Penny is the gold standard for the modern literary cozy mystery. The writing is better than genre fiction usually bothers to be, the village of Three Pines feels utterly real after a few books, and Gamache himself is one of the most fully realised detective protagonists in the genre. Start with *Still Life* and give it two books before you judge, the series improves considerably as the world deepens.

### Alan Bradley — Flavia de Luce series (from 2009)

Flavia de Luce is eleven years old, lives in a crumbling English manor house, is obsessed with poison chemistry, and is the most brilliant detective in the county. The series is set in 1950s rural England and written with tremendous wit and period detail.

*The Sweetness at the Bottom of the Pie* is the opener and one of the most purely enjoyable crime novels of the last twenty years. Flavia is an unforgettable creation — precocious without being irritating, sharp without being cold.

### Nita Prose — The Maid (2022)

Molly the Maid is a hotel housekeeper with a precise, rule-governed way of seeing the world, and she discovers a dead body in one of her rooms. A cozy mystery with an unusually tender approach to its protagonist, and a genuine emotional depth that elevates it above most of the genre.

### Jesse Q. Sutanto — Vera Wong's Unsolicited Advice for Murderers (2023)

Vera Wong is a middle-aged Chinese-American woman who runs a San Francisco tea house and has decided, entirely on her own initiative, to investigate a murder that happened on her premises. She is meddlesome, opinionated, hilarious, and ultimately right about everything.

One of the funniest cozy mysteries published in recent years, and a genuine antidote to the genteel English-village template. Vera's voice is completely its own.

### Robert Thorogood — The Marlow Murder Club (from 2021)

Judith Potts is 77, lives alone in a large house on the Thames, and has just witnessed a murder, she thinks. Nobody believes her. So she investigates herself, along with a dog-walker and a vicar's wife she recruits along the way.

Written by the creator of the *Death in Paradise* TV series, this has exactly the cosy, community spirit of the best of the genre. The ensemble cast is warm and funny, and the mysteries are properly plotted. A natural companion to the Thursday Murder Club for readers who want more elderly amateur sleuths.

### M.C. Beaton — Agatha Raisin series (from 1992)

Agatha Raisin is a retired PR executive who moves to the Cotswolds expecting peace and quiet and gets murder instead, repeatedly. She is blunt, impetuous, socially awkward, and completely irresistible.

Beaton was one of the most prolific writers of cozy mysteries in the modern era, and the Agatha Raisin series captures the village England setting at its most absurd and charming. *The Quiche of Death* is the opener and a good test of whether the tone works for you.

## Where to Start

**New to cozy mystery?** Start with *The Thursday Murder Club* if you want something contemporary, or *A Murder is Announced* if you want the classic Christie experience.

**Want a long series to sink into?** Louise Penny's Gamache books will last you years, and they get better as you go.

**Want something different in tone?** *Vera Wong* if you want funny, *The No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency* if you want warm and philosophical.
