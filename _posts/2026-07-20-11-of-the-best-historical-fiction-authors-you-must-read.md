Historical fiction has something of a reputation problem. A lot of people treat historical fiction as a sensible read, a genre you tackle when you want to feel a bit more productive or a bit more high-brow about your reading time. That reputation can be, I think a bit off-putting. No one likes homework, after all. 

However, I also want to say, that reputation is wrong. Sure a badly writtin historical fantasy novel can be dry as bone and as dense as any literary tome. But, great story-telling transcends genre. 

What the best historical fiction authors share is restraint. They research a period until they know it cold, then bury almost all of that research so the story never feels like work. What's left on the page is people making choices, with real consequences, against events that actually happened. The world-building and characters and events become more grounded, the stakes higher, the tension heightened because of the genre not in spite of it.

I've pulled together 15 authors who do this well, across very different registers. Some of these books you'll finish in a weekend. Others ask more of you and pay it back with interest.

## New To Historical Fiction? Start Here

If you want something fast that pulls you straight into a period, start with Bernard Cornwell or Conn Iggulden. If you want something more literary that will sit with you for a while after you finish it, Hilary Mantel or Colm Toibin are better entry points. If romance is doing a lot of the work for you, Diana Gabaldon or Beatriz Williams.

There's no wrong place to begin. The genre covers such a wide range of tone that your first pick says more about what you want from a book than it does about historical fiction itself.

    <h3>1. Bernard Cornwell - The Last Kingdom</h3>
    <p>Bernard Cornwell has spent five decades writing about men fighting for survival on muddy, bloody battlefields, and doing it better than most people in the genre. His Sharpe series follows Richard Sharpe (played by a young Sean Bean in the TV adaptation if that's your cup of tea) through the Napoleonic Wars across 22 books, so once you start there's no shortage of reading ahead of you.</p>
    <p>The Last Kingdom series is probably his best known work now thanks to the recent Netflix adaptation, and it holds up on the page even if you've already watched Uhtred fight his way across Britain. Cornwell writes battles with a level of tactical clarity that most historical fiction authors don't attempt. You can actually follow what's happening and why.</p>
    <p><strong>Connect with:</strong> The Warlord Chronicles, his Arthurian trilogy and arguably his best work, quieter and more melancholic than the Saxon Stories. Or the Sharpe series if you want to move forward a few centuries.</p>

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is historical fiction based on true events?

Usually, yes, at least in part. The best historical fiction authors set invented characters and plots against real events, settings, and sometimes real historical figures, and they do the research to get the texture of the period right. The plot itself, though, is fiction. A historical novel isn't a textbook, and it shouldn't be read as one.

### What's the difference between historical fiction and historical fantasy?

Historical fiction stays within what actually happened or could plausibly have happened. Historical fantasy borrows the setting and texture of a real period but adds magic, gods, or invented history on top. Diana Gabaldon's Outlander sits close to that line with its time travel, but the 18th century Scotland she writes is otherwise grounded in real history, which is why it's usually shelved as historical fiction rather than fantasy.

### Do I need to read these authors' books in order?

Most of the authors on this list write standalone novels or loosely connected series, so you can generally start wherever a premise grabs you. The exceptions are trilogies with a single continuous story, like Hilary Mantel's Cromwell books or Mary Renault's Alexander novels, which are worth reading in publication order.

### Which of these authors should I read first?

If you're new to the genre, Bernard Cornwell or Conn Iggulden give you the fastest, most immediate entry point. If you want to understand why critics take historical fiction seriously as literature, start with Hilary Mantel.

## Final Words

There's no single best historical fiction author, because the genre covers too much ground for one answer to make sense. What connects everyone on this list is a refusal to let research get in the way of story, and a willingness to make you feel something about people who, in most cases, never actually existed.

Start with whichever one matches what you're in the mood for. You'll find your way to the rest eventually.
